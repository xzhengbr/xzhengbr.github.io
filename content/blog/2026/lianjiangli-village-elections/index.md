## Reading Harrop, Miller, and Li

In Chapter 8, Harrop and Miller examine how short-run political factors generate deviations around long-term patterns of party support. They argue that while social structure and partisan loyalties set a baseline, variables such as the economy, mass media, party leaders, campaigns, canvassing, money, and opinion polls explain much of the remaining variation.

They show that macroeconomic conditions, especially unemployment, real disposable income, and inflation, account for 20-40% of the fluctuation in government popularity, with voters focusing myopically on the recent past and often punishing incumbents for any economic downturn. The media, particularly television, shape what voters think about through agenda setting, personalize politics by emphasizing leaders, and modestly erode traditional party loyalties. Campaigns are portrayed as "end-games at chess": they mainly review government performance, mobilize existing support, and rarely transform outcomes, though they can be decisive at the margins or under proportional representation. Canvassing slightly boosts turnout, money helps challengers be heard more than it directly buys votes, and opinion polls have small, mostly indirect effects. The chapter closes by calling for a genuinely political theory of voting that integrates these short-term influences.

Compared with Harrop and Miller, Lianjiang Li (2003) adopts a more optimistic view of what elections can achieve, especially their potential to enhance political efficacy. Li (2003) investigates whether introducing genuinely competitive village committee elections increases Chinese villagers' sense of external political efficacy, meaning the belief that they can influence officials' behaviour. He conducts a two-wave panel survey of 400 villagers in 20 Jiangxi province villages before and after their first procedurally free and fair election in 1999, documenting a major procedural shift from tightly controlled or indirect selections to direct nomination, secret ballots, contested elections, and open counting. He focuses on what villagers say they would do if village or township authorities fail to implement beneficial central policies. After the election, significantly more respondents say they would refuse to vote for noncompliant cadres, persuade others to do the same, initiate impeachment, or press the village committee to challenge unlawful township decisions.

Confirmatory factor analysis shows these items load on a single latent "external efficacy" dimension, whose mean rises by about 0.57 standard deviations after the election. Li (2003) argues this enhanced efficacy may spur greater political participation, embolden elected committee directors to confront party secretaries and township governments, and, over time, help restructure village power relations. He cautions, however, that the durability and generalizability of this empowerment require further study.

## Remaining Issues

Regarding Li's article, several issues remain about how fragile and context-dependent this effect may be. Using Harrop and Miller's Chapter 8 as a benchmark on short-term electoral influences helps highlight these unresolved questions.

**First, the finding rests on a very short observation window**: a two-wave panel conducted just before and shortly after a single election, with the post-election survey completed within weeks of balloting. Harrop and Miller show that electorates are myopic, heavily weighting only the recent past in their political judgements. In T county, villagers had just experienced their first-ever free and fair election. The electoral process itself, including secret balloting, public vote counting, and direct nomination by villagers, carries a strong sense of ceremonial significance, which may create a temporary sense of empowerment regardless of whether the elected officials are truly responsive. This raises the possibility that Li is capturing a transient "electoral honeymoon" uplift in efficacy, rather than a durable and robust change in villagers' sense of empowerment.

**Second, the indicators used to measure external efficacy are hypothetical intention items** that tap expectations about future behaviour rather than evaluations grounded in actual experience. Harrop and Miller stress that opinion-poll responses are more volatile and easier to move than actual votes. This suggests Li may overstate the substantive impact of the observed 0.57-unit increase in external efficacy without evidence that villagers actually follow through in later elections or confrontations. Given the very short window between election and post-election survey, the newly elected committees had barely had time to face any real policy-implementation tests.

**Third, the panel design introduces an important, unmodelled source of reactivity.** Li notes that interviewers "had to stay in the villages for an extended period of time to administer the surveys and observe elections," which may have enabled them to develop "personal rapport" with respondents. By the second wave, villagers may have had a clearer sense of the study's normative orientation and of what answers would appear "appropriate" to outside researchers and higher authorities. Under such conditions, social desirability bias and broader reactivity effects can systematically tilt responses without any necessary change.

**Furthermore, in an authoritarian environment there is a structural gap between the intention to speak out and the actions actually taken.** Even if villagers feel they have the right to recall officials, the social pressures they face in practice, such as the risk of retaliation, dense local acquaintance networks, and collective-action dilemmas, may lead them to remain silent. Even if a subjective sense of efficacy genuinely exists, whether it can be translated into sustained participation remains an issue that Li's paper does not address.

## Possible Methodological Responses

To distinguish between a short-term "post-election honeymoon" and a longer-term political efficacy, several design improvements can be adopted.

**First, multi-wave panel surveys should be used.** An ideal design would involve at least three waves: a pre-election baseline, a survey approximately 6-8 weeks after the election to capture immediate effects, and a follow-up 12-18 months later to test persistence. By comparing external efficacy across these time points, researchers can observe whether perceived efficacy declines, stabilizes, or continues to rise. A substantial decline in the third wave would support a honeymoon interpretation, whereas stable or increasing levels would be more consistent with a lasting institutional effect. This design requires careful attention to major intervening events, sample attrition, and data quality.

**Second, a cross-village difference-in-differences design can be employed.** Villages that introduce free and fair elections serve as the treatment group, while similar villages that have not yet implemented such reforms, or where elections remain low-quality, serve as controls. Repeated surveys in both groups allow researchers to exploit a longer observation window while netting out common shocks, such as macroeconomic changes or national propaganda campaigns, which affect both sets of villages. The main challenge is identifying sufficiently comparable control villages.

**Third, repeated measurement across election cycles can be used.** Tracking two or three rounds of village committee elections within the same county makes it possible to compare the size and durability of external efficacy changes after the first, second, and third rounds, and thus to determine whether effects dissipate, accumulate, or are unique to the initial introduction of elections.

To measure external efficacy more accurately, it is important to move from intentions to behaviour and performance. Survey items of the form "if ... would you ..." should be complemented with behavioural indicators and administrative data, such as changes in turnout, the incidence of recall motions, joint petitions, collective appeals, and the frequency and outcomes of formal negotiations between village committees and township governments. In addition, researchers can trace specific cases of central policy implementation, such as land compensation, to see whether villagers actually invoke elections, collective action, or negotiations to defend their interests. This links institutional "promises" to institutional "performance."

To mitigate social desirability bias and reactivity, several methodological tools are available. Subsequent waves can include a refresh sample of newly drawn respondents from the same villages; comparing them with panel members helps estimate panel conditioning. For politically sensitive questions, such as recalling officials, list experiments or randomized response techniques can reduce social desirability bias. Random assignment of interviewers to villages and waves, combined with standardized training and scripts, can further limit interviewer-specific rapport effects and implicit cues.

Finally, if a short observation window cannot be avoided, the theoretical scope of Li's claims should be explicitly restricted. In that case, conclusions ought to be framed as "free elections significantly enhance villagers' sense of external efficacy in the short term," rather than as evidence of a durable institutional transformation. This highlights a classic trade-off between internal and external validity: shorter windows sharpen causal identification of immediate effects, while longer ones better capture real political dynamics.
