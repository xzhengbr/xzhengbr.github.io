## Abstract

Hong Kong's Muslim population has surged by 87% since 2001, yet the city relies on six mosques, five built before 1981, that are spatially concentrated in Hong Kong Island and Kowloon. This mismatch creates severe service gaps in the New Territories, forcing 15,000 Indonesian domestic workers to use improvised prayer sites. Through GIS-based spatial analysis, this study identifies optimal locations for Hong Kong's seventh mosque by integrating proxy demographic data, facility distribution, and urban planning criteria. A Mosque Demand Index, weighting population density, uncovered populations, growth trends from 2011 to 2021, and proximity to existing mosques, reveals Yuen Long (65/72), Sha Tin (56/72), and Tuen Mun (54/72) as priority districts. Site suitability analysis of "Government, Institution or Community" (GIC) zoned land further narrows candidate parcels: Yuen Long (S/YL/27) and Sha Tin (S/ST/38). The Sha Tin site demonstrates the highest potential, newly covering 15,814 Muslims within a 5 km radius. Beyond infrastructure pressure, the study frames the mosque as essential for social equity, addressing language barriers and discrimination against non-Chinese Muslims, and reinforcing Hong Kong's global competitiveness amid deficits in halal infrastructure. Policy recommendations include streamlined land allocation, interfaith engagement, and integration with Northern Metropolis development. This research provides data-driven solutions to reconcile demographic realities with Hong Kong's commitment to religious pluralism.

## Background

The diverse ethnic composition of Hong Kong's Muslim community is closely tied to the city's colonial history and the socio-economic developments of different periods. The ancestors of South Asian Muslims can be traced back to the early 1840s, when Hong Kong was first established as a colony. Muslim soldiers, police officers, prison guards, merchants, and sailors from India, who were part of the British Empire at the time, arrived in Hong Kong alongside the British. It was they who first introduced Islam to the region. During the early years of Hong Kong's establishment, as various ethnic groups collaborated to transform the once-obscure fishing village into an international port linking East and West, Indian Muslims played a significant role across various professions. Given their large numbers and close ties with the colonial government, they were granted government support, enabling them to quickly establish dedicated religious facilities such as mosques.

Today's Muslims in Hong Kong mainly originate from Indonesia, Pakistan, India, and Bangladesh. Indonesia is the world's most populous Muslim country, and most Indonesian domestic workers in Hong Kong are Muslims. Yet this vibrancy is constrained by a severe spatial crisis. There are a total of six mosques in this city. Five of them were built before 1981, and only one new mosque was added in 2013. Besides, the distribution of mosques is concentrated in Hong Kong Island and Kowloon, leaving New Territories communities underserved. Meanwhile, the Muslim population has surged by 87% since 2001, overwhelming existing infrastructure.

In this context, a seventh mosque is not merely beneficial. It is a necessary corrective to decades of planning inertia, essential to uphold Hong Kong's legal promises of religious equity and integrate its marginalized communities into the city's future.

## Demand Analysis

This paper will analyze the demand for Hong Kong's seventh mosque from three aspects: Muslim population growth and facility pressure, social equity and religious inclusiveness, and Hong Kong's position as an international metropolis.

### Population Growth and Infrastructure Pressure

According to the Hong Kong Government's 2001 and 2022 reports[^1], there were an estimated 80,000 and 300,000 Muslims respectively. Over the past 20 years, the number of Muslims has increased by 250%, but only one new mosque has been built.

The existing six mosques are as follows:

1. Caine Road Mosque

The Caine Road Mosque in the Mid-Levels of Hong Kong Island is the oldest mosque in the city. Built in 1849, it was reconstructed in 1915 and listed as a declared monument in 2022.

2. Al-Azhar Mosque

The eight-storey Al-Azhar Mosque and Lin Sik Tak Islamic Centre in Wan Chai houses multiple facilities, including a library, clinic, restaurant, and kindergarten.

3. Kowloon Mosque

Constructed with dolomite for both its interior and exterior walls, the Kowloon Mosque and Islamic Centre stands as a landmark in the Tsim Sha Tsui area. Capable of accommodating 3,500 worshippers, it is used by nearly 1 million people each year.

4. Stanley Mosque

The Stanley Mosque is a mosque in Stanley. It is the fourth mosque built in Hong Kong and is located at Stanley Prison.

5. Chai Wan Mosque

The Chai Wan Mosque, or Cape Collinson Mosque, is a mosque in Chai Wan. It is the fifth mosque built in Hong Kong.

6. Ibrahim Mosque

The Ibrahim Mosque is a mosque in Mong Kok. It is the sixth and latest mosque built in Hong Kong. The mosque was constructed and is managed by the United Welfare Union Hong Kong Limited.

Six mosques are located in Central and Western, Wan Chai, Eastern, Southern, and Yau Tsim Mong. These only account for 5 of Hong Kong's 18 districts. Meanwhile, an estimated 15,000 Indonesian domestic helpers resort to makeshift prayer sites in parks and footbridges due to geographical and temporal inaccessibility of formal facilities.

### Social Equity and Religious Inclusiveness

Hong Kong's Muslim community, particularly non-Chinese ethnic minorities such as South Asians and Indonesians, faces significant challenges in social integration and access to equitable resources. Many non-Chinese Muslim students struggle with limited Chinese language proficiency, which restricts their educational and career opportunities. Implicit discrimination and negative perceptions toward South Asians, often framing Muslims as "outsiders," are also reported, as noted in *Islam in Hong Kong: Muslims and Everyday Life in China's World City* by Paul O'Connor.[^2] A new mosque, especially in underserved areas like the New Territories, would provide a dedicated space for worship and community activities, fostering social inclusion by addressing these disparities. It could also support educational initiatives, such as Quranic classes and cultural programs, which are currently limited by space constraints.

Religious inclusiveness is another critical aspect. Hong Kong's Basic Law guarantees religious freedom, and the city prides itself on cultural pluralism. However, the limited number of mosques compared to the prevalence of Buddhist and Taoist temples suggests an imbalance in accommodating minority religions. For instance, Kowloon Mosque serves as a vital cultural and religious hub, promoting coexistence through events like Masjid Open Day, but its capacity is stretched. An incident in 2019, where the mosque was accidentally sprayed with blue dye during protests, demonstrated community solidarity, with volunteers from various backgrounds helping to clean it, reflecting Hong Kong's potential for inclusiveness. Building a new mosque would reinforce this commitment, ensuring Muslims have equitable access to religious spaces and signaling to the broader society that minority faiths are valued.

The Muslim community includes diverse groups, such as women, youth, and migrant workers, who face unique challenges. Female Muslim converts, for example, navigate cultural and religious identities in a predominantly Chinese society, often requiring spaces for religious education and community support. A new mosque could incorporate facilities like women's prayer areas, Islamic kindergartens, and libraries, addressing these needs and promoting inclusivity across gender and ethnicity, as seen in existing mosques like Kowloon Mosque, which includes madrasahs and community halls.

### Hong Kong's Position as an International Metropolis

Hong Kong markets itself as "Asia's World City," characterized by cultural diversity, economic vitality, and global connectivity. However, it lags behind other global cities in providing Muslim-friendly infrastructure. For example, Tokyo offers accessible prayer rooms and ablution facilities in shopping centers, while Hong Kong has only 1.9% of its 17,500 eateries halal-certified and 6.5% of its 321 hotels with a Muslim-friendly Crescent Rating. This gap limits its appeal to Muslim residents and visitors, despite its significant Muslim population and status as a top global destination attracting millions annually, including from Southeast Asia, the Middle East, and beyond.

A new mosque, strategically located in a high-traffic area or near tourist hubs, could enhance Hong Kong's reputation as a Muslim-friendly destination, boosting tourism and supporting local businesses, such as halal food stalls near existing mosques like those at Chungking Mansions.

Constructing a new mosque in Hong Kong is essential to address the growing Muslim population's needs, alleviate pressure on existing facilities, and promote social equity and religious inclusiveness. It would also enhance Hong Kong's position as a leading international metropolis by catering to its diverse population and attracting more Muslim tourists and residents. This initiative would not only serve the Muslim community but also reinforce Hong Kong's reputation as a model of multicultural coexistence.

## Site Selection Analysis

### Data

#### Proxy Data on the Muslim Population

According to the Hong Kong Government's 2022 report[^3], there are an estimated 300,000 Muslims. However, since the Hong Kong census data does not include faith statistics, according to the estimated data of the Hong Kong Islamic Trust Fund[^4], about 60% of Hong Kong's Muslims are Indonesians and Pakistanis, with about 150,000 Indonesians, or 50.00%, and 30,000 Pakistanis, or 16.67%, of the total.

This paper uses publicly available data from DATA.GOV.HK.[^5] It is an open data pool with various comprehensive data available for public use, and the data of "Hong Kong Population Distribution by Ethnicity by 18 Districts in 2021" and the data of "Population Distribution by Ethnicity in 2011" are spatially enabled.

According to the Hong Kong 2021 Population Census[^6], there are 142,065 Indonesians and 24,385 Pakistanis in Hong Kong in 2021, totaling 166,450, which is more consistent with the estimated number of Muslims. Therefore, in the subsequent spatial analysis, this paper uses the combined value of Indonesians and Pakistanis as a proxy variable for the distribution of the Muslim population in Hong Kong, named Hong Kong Muslim Minority Population.

#### Mosque Location Data

Based on the data from Wikipedia's "List of Mosques in Hong Kong," the Hong Kong Government Annual Report 2022, General Association of the Hong Kong Islamic Religious Trust, and Google Maps, this paper created a new shapefile in ArcGIS Pro with the type of point, and selected the coordinate system of Hong Kong 1980 Grid System to mark the locations of the six mosques. The locations of the six mosques are labeled on the map in order.

#### Planning Data of Hong Kong Districts

The Outline Zoning Plans of the Town Planning Board of Hong Kong[^7] will be used for site selection in specific districts. The Board is a statutory body of Hong Kong and provides Outline Zoning Plans (OZPs) and Development Permission Area (DPA) Plans for the layout of various areas of Hong Kong.

Sites suitable for planning a mosque are identified as Government, Institution or Community (GIC) in accordance with the Explanatory Statement and Schedule of Uses.[^8] This type of site is applied for religious institutions as "Uses always permitted" or "Uses that may be permitted with or without conditions on application to the Town Planning Board."

### Methods

#### Mosque Demand Index

First, centered on the six existing mosques, in ArcGIS Pro, choose Analysis > Tools > Buffer, with distance selected as 5 kilometers, getting a 5-kilometer radius centered on each mosque. Then select Analysis > Tools > Intersect to combine the radiation area for the population data and the administrative district, which contains the Muslim population that can be covered by a mosque.

Next, a comprehensive Mosque Demand Index was constructed to objectively assess the demand for new mosques in the 18 districts of Hong Kong. This index consists of four indicators: the Muslim minority population in 2021, the Muslim minority population not covered by mosques in 2021, the growth trend of the Muslim minority population from 2011 to 2021, and the closest distance to any existing mosque.

**Indicator 1: Muslim minority population, 2021**

The 2021 Muslim minority population in each district is used to measure the extent to which there is a current demand for mosques in each district, with higher values indicating a higher demand for mosques.

The population of Muslim minorities, Indonesians and Pakistanis, in Hong Kong by district in 2021 is shown in Column "Indicator 1" of Table 1. The numbers are ranked in descending order and assigned values, with the most populous district being assigned 18 points, the next 17 points, and so on, and the least populous district being assigned 1 point.

**Indicator 2: Muslim minority population not covered by a mosque, 2021**

Indicator 2 excludes Muslims already covered by existing mosques, emphasizing the number of people in the gap. The higher the number, the greater the need for mosques. Calculate the population of Muslim minorities not covered by existing mosques in each district according to the Attribute Table. Shown in Column "Indicator 2" of Table 1, the number of people was sorted in descending order and assigned a value, where the district with the largest uncovered population was assigned 18 points, followed by 17 points, and so on, and the district with the smallest number of people was assigned 1 point.

**Indicator 3: Trends in the growth of the Muslim minority population, 2011-2021**

The growth trend measures the development of the Muslim population in each district over the last 10 years. The faster the growth rate, the larger the future Muslim population and the greater the demand for mosques.

The growth rate of the Muslim minority population in each district is shown in Column "Indicator 3" of Table 1. The districts are ranked according to the rate of growth from the highest to the lowest and assigned a value of 18 points for the highest rate of growth, 17 points for the second highest rate, and so on, with the lowest district being assigned a value of 1 point.

**Indicator 4: Districts' closest distance to any existing mosque**

Indicator 4 measures how difficult it is for Muslims in the districts to get to existing mosques; the greater the distance and the more difficult the commute, the greater the need for a new mosque within the district.

In ArcGIS Pro, select Analysis > Tools > Feature To Point to convert each zone polygon feature into a point, or centroid, then select Analysis > Tools > Near. The districts were ranked in order of distance from the mosque and assigned a value in that order, with the furthest distance being assigned 18 points, the next 17 points, and so on, and the closest district being assigned 1 point, as shown in Column "Indicator 4" of Table 1.

Combining the above four indicators and summing them with equal weights, a score out of 72 was obtained for the Mosque Demand Index in each district. Among them, Yuen Long (65), Sha Tin (56), and Tuen Mun (54) are among the top three districts which can be considered as candidate districts. From the perspective of demand, the seventh mosque in Hong Kong should be prioritized in Yuen Long, Sha Tin, or Tuen Mun.

## Policy Recommendations

Hong Kong's Muslim community has grown significantly, straining the six existing mosques. A new mosque in Hong Kong could ease this pressure, providing accessible worship spaces. A new mosque would support non-Chinese Muslims, who often face language barriers and discrimination, by offering a space for worship and community activities. It would also signal Hong Kong's commitment to religious freedom, ensuring equitable access to religious facilities in a city with many Buddhist and Taoist temples.

Building a mosque could make Hong Kong more attractive to Muslim tourists and residents, boosting tourism and local businesses. With only 1.9% of eateries halal-certified, a new mosque could anchor broader Muslim-friendly infrastructure, aligning with Hong Kong's "Asia's World City" branding.

The government should allocate land in the New Territories, streamline approvals, and offer financial incentives for mosque construction. Engaging Muslim organizations and promoting interfaith dialogue will ensure community support and inclusivity.

1. Formal Recognition: Issue a policy statement acknowledging the Muslim community's growth and the need for additional mosques to ensure equitable religious access.
2. Land Allocation: Allocate land in the New Territories, prioritizing sites near public transport to serve residents and visitors.
3. Streamlined Approvals: Simplify land acquisition, permits, and funding processes, addressing delays seen in the Sheung Shui project.
4. Financial Support: Offer grants, subsidies, or tax incentives and encourage public-private partnerships to fund construction.
5. Inclusive Design: Engage Muslim organizations like the Islamic Community Fund to design facilities for women, youth, and education.
6. Community Engagement: Promote interfaith dialogue and public awareness to build support and counter opposition.
7. Muslim-Friendly Infrastructure: Integrate the mosque with initiatives to increase halal dining and prayer facilities in public spaces.

## Conclusion

This study substantiates the urgent need for Hong Kong's seventh mosque through rigorous spatial and socio-demographic analysis. The 87% growth in Muslims since 2001, driven by Indonesian domestic workers and South Asian immigrants, has overwhelmed colonial-era infrastructure, with Kowloon Mosque accommodating more than 30,000 worshippers during Eid against a 3,500-person capacity. The GIS-driven Mosque Demand Index and site suitability assessment prioritize Yuen Long (S/YL/27) and Sha Tin (S/ST/38) based on uncovered populations, growth trajectories, and GIC zoning compliance. Critically, a Sha Tin mosque would newly serve 15,814 Muslims, enhancing accessibility for marginalized communities in the underserved New Territories.

The project transcends religious infrastructure: it addresses systemic inequities for non-Chinese Muslims facing linguistic exclusion and implicit bias while advancing Hong Kong's branding as "Asia's World City." With only 6.5% of hotels rated Muslim-friendly, the mosque could anchor broader initiatives, including halal tourism and Islamic finance hubs, to counter regional competitors like Singapore. Policy imperatives include fast-tracking GIC land transfers, cross-sectoral funding models, and co-designing facilities such as women's prayer spaces and libraries with the Islamic Community Fund.

Future research should integrate temporal mobility data, such as domestic workers' Sunday movements, and evaluate socio-cultural impacts through community ethnography. Nevertheless, this analysis delivers actionable blueprints for spatial justice. By situating the mosque within Northern Metropolis development and interfaith dialogue frameworks, Hong Kong can transform demographic pressures into a testament of inclusive urbanism, fulfilling its Basic Law mandate while modeling global-city pluralism.
