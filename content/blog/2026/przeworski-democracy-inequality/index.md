## Reading Przeworski et al.

Przeworski et al.'s (1996) "What Makes Democracies Endure?" examines the conditions under which democratic regimes survive, drawing on data from 135 countries between 1950 and 1990, covering 4,318 country-years and 224 regimes.

**The study's central finding is the important role of economic factors in sustaining democracies.** On wealth, the authors find a stark income threshold effect. Poor democracies with per-capita income below \$1,000 face a 0.12 annual probability of collapse, giving them an average life expectancy of just 8.5 years. Above \$6,000, no democracy has ever fallen; they become effectively permanent. Crucially, the authors refute the influential argument that dictatorships are better at generating development in poor countries: dictatorships show no advantage over democracies in producing economic growth, and transitions to democracy are statistically unrelated to income level, undermining any justification for tolerating authoritarianism as a path to development.

For less affluent countries where wealth alone cannot guarantee survival, democratic durability depends on comprehensive conditions: existing democracy, affluence, economic growth with moderate inflation, declining inequality, a favorable international climate, and parliamentary institutions.

**On economic performance**, rapid growth and low inflation significantly boost democratic survival, while economic crisis, particularly income decline, is among the most common triggers of democratic breakdown. Democracies experiencing falling incomes have a life expectancy of under five years at low-income levels.

**On inequality**, democracies where income inequality is declining survive around 84 years on average, compared to just 22 years where inequality is rising, suggesting citizens expect democracy to deliver distributive progress.

**On the international climate**, the authors find that the global and regional density of democracies powerfully shapes regime survival: the higher the proportion of democracies in the world and in a country's region in a given year, the more likely democracy is to endure there, with the global effect roughly twice as large as the regional one. This operates as a form of diffusion or "contagion" that cannot be reduced to direct Western pressure, since the statistical effect remains even after accounting for formal external influences.

**On political learning**, they argue that past democratic experience cuts both ways. Countries with prior democratic episodes do not just inherit democratic traditions; they also inherit successful recipes for subverting democracy. Any past overthrow of democracy shortens the expected life of subsequent democratic regimes, implying that anti-democratic actors learn more effectively from previous breakdowns than democrats benefit from "democratic traditions." As a result, repeated breakdowns make future democracies more, not less, fragile.

**On institutions**, parliamentary systems dramatically outperform presidential ones: democracy's expected life under presidentialism is under 20 years, versus 71 years under parliamentarism. Presidential systems are especially fragile when combined with fragmented legislatures, where executive-legislative deadlock cuts expected democratic life to just 11 years.

Together, these findings reframe democratic consolidation as fundamentally shaped by structural economic conditions and institutional design, rather than political will or elite bargaining alone.

## Remaining Issues

When focusing on the impact of inequality on democratic resilience, there are some remaining issues that need to be addressed.

First, **data quality and coverage** remain a fundamental obstacle. Przeworski et al. explicitly note that the analysis of inequality's political effects is "hampered by the paucity and poor quality of the available data," with only 266 observations across 84 countries drawn from the World Bank's collection. Such sparse, noisy coverage makes it difficult to know whether the observed absence of a clear global relationship between inequality levels and democratic survival reflects a true effect or simply measurement error and sample limitations.

Second, there are potential **confounding variables and endogeneity issues in economic development.** Inequality tends to be relatively low in both very poor agrarian economies and very rich wage-earning economies, while democracy is fragile in the former and effectively impregnable in the latter. Because the inequality data are too scant to allow robust controls for income levels and growth, any apparent link between inequality and survival risks conflating the effect of distribution with the effect of development. At the same time, democracy itself can reshape inequality through taxation, social spending, and labor regulation. The striking result that democracies with declining inequality have an expected life of 84 years versus 22 years when inequality rises raises, but does not resolve, the question of whether reducing inequality causes greater democratic durability or is primarily a consequence of already stable democracies.

Third, the study's use of **a single top-bottom quintile ratio** as the inequality metric raises a conceptual issue: it may be too crude to capture the politically salient structure of the income distribution. Many theories of democratic stability focus on the size and security of the middle class, the intensity of polarization, or specific cleavages such as rural-urban or ethnic income gaps. Collapsing the entire distribution into one ratio between the richest and poorest quintiles risks missing the mechanisms through which distributive tensions translate into regime outcomes. The authors themselves concede that no "overall pattern" emerges from their level-of-inequality analysis, in part because they cannot control adequately for development; but even with better controls, a single ratio may fail to capture the aspects of inequality most relevant for democratic breakdown or survival.

## Possible Methodological Responses

To tackle the data quality and coverage problem, future research should exploit the much richer inequality datasets now available. Harmonized panel series such as SWIID, WIID, standardized Gini indices, and micro-data from sources like the Luxembourg Income Study can greatly expand the temporal and cross-national coverage beyond the 266 observations that constrained Przeworski et al. Using these sources would sharply reduce measurement error and allow replication of their survival analysis with far more observations, testing whether their core finding, that democracies with declining inequality live dramatically longer, holds up under improved data.

Addressing confounding and endogeneity with development requires more sophisticated empirical strategies. A natural extension of Przeworski et al.'s own design is to estimate multivariate survival, or hazard, models in which democratic breakdown is the event and inequality enters as a time-varying covariate alongside GDP per capita, growth, inflation, and other controls. This would allow explicit conditioning on development and macroeconomic performance, reducing the risk that inequality coefficients are simply picking up income effects. Within-country panel approaches with country fixed effects can further difference out time-invariant national characteristics, such as culture or colonial heritage, so that identification comes from changes in inequality over time within the same country. To address endogeneity, researchers can lag inequality measures, use instrumental variables based on exogenous shocks to distribution, or apply dynamic panel methods that jointly model democracy and inequality while accounting for feedback. These techniques cannot fully eliminate causal ambiguity, but they move well beyond the descriptive constraints acknowledged in the original article.

Finally, to remedy the over-simple inequality metric, we should adopt a richer set of distributional measures that better map onto theories of democratic stability. Alongside the rich-poor quintile ratio, this might include overall Gini coefficients, measures of income polarization, the income share of the middle 60 percent, or subgroup-specific indicators that capture rural-urban, regional, or ethnic income gaps. With such measures in hand, one can test more precise hypotheses: for example, whether democracies are particularly vulnerable when the middle class shrinks, when regional disparities widen, or when elite incomes pull far ahead of the rest without commensurate gains for the median voter. Embedding these refined indicators into hazard models and combining quantitative results with targeted case studies of democratic crises under shifting inequality patterns would help illuminate whether it is inequality per se, specific distributional configurations, or their interaction with institutions that most threatens democratic endurance.
